In the symmetric group \(S_n\), an adjacent transposition is a \(2\)-cycle where the indices differ by \(1\), which we write as \(s_i = (i\ i+1)\). For instance, \(s_2 = (2\ 3)\) or \(s_8 = (8\ 9)\) are adjacent transpositions, but \((1\ 9)\) is not. It turns out that every permutation in \(S_n\) can be written as a product of transpositions.

  1. [1] Write the word \(524316\) as a product of adjacent transpositions. (Hint: what is the first letter of \(s_1 \circ s_2 \circ s_3 \circ s_4\) written as a word?)
  2. [1] Check this result in WolframAlpha by inputting permutation (1 2)(2 3) but with the transpositions found above. (Note that WolframAlpha reads the permutations left-to-right!)
  3. [3] If a permutation is written as a sequence of adjacent transpositions and there is no way to write it as a shorter sequence adjacent transpositions, then the sequence of adjacent transpositions is called a reduced word. (Note: a reduced word is a product of transpositions, not to be confused with writing a permutation in word notation.) We can move between reduced words by using the two identities:
    1. \(s_i \circ s_j = s_j \circ s_i\) if \(|i - j| \geq 2\)
    2. \(s_i \circ s_{i+1} \circ s_i = s_{i+1} \circ s_i \circ s_{i+1}\) for all \(1 \leq i < n - 1\).
    Prove these two identities, and use them to give an example of a reduced word that is different from the reduced word \( s_1 \circ s_3 \circ s_2 \circ s_3 \circ s_4 \circ s_3 \circ s_2 \circ s_1 \) but which is equal as a permutation.
  4. [5] Prove that every permutation in \(S_n\) can be written as a product of adjacent transpositions, and thus \[ \langle s_1, s_2, \dots, s_{n-1} \rangle = S_n. \]
  5. [3] While \(S_6\) cannot be generated by a single element (because it is not a cyclic group), it can be generated by just two elements: \[ \langle (1\ 2), \underbrace{(1\ 2\ 3\ 4\ 5\ 6)}_c \rangle = S_6. \] Write the word \(524316\) in terms of \(s_1\) and \(c\).
